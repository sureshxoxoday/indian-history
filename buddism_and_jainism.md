## 1. 6TH CENTURY BCE BACKDROP — WHY NEW RELIGIONS EMERGED

- 6th century BCE = age of global religious upheaval — **Confucius** (China), **Zoroaster** (Iran), **Parmenides** (Greece) all questioned established socio-religious norms
- In India → produced two great **heterodox religions**: Buddhism and Jainism
- Core teaching: true happiness lies NOT in material wealth or ritual, but in **non-violence, charity, frugality and good conduct**

### Causes of Origin (High-Yield for Mains)

| Cause | Explanation |
|-------|-------------|
| **(a) Kshatriya Reaction** | Post-Vedic society had 4 varnas; Kshatriyas ranked 2nd below Brahmanas and resented Brahmanical privileges (gifts, tax & punishment exemption). Both Buddha and Mahavira were Kshatriyas. Pali texts **reverse the rank order** placing Kshatriya above Brahmana |
| **(b) New Agrarian Economy** | Economic centre shifted west→east (Haryana/W-UP → E-UP & Bihar) for more rainfall, fertile land and iron ore. Iron ploughshare farming needed bullocks, so Vedic **animal sacrifice (cattle slaughter) blocked progress**. Buddhism/Jainism preached non-violence → appealed to peasants |
| **(c) Vaishya/Mercantile Patronage** | 6th c. BCE = **Second Urbanisation**; 60+ towns, thousands of silver/copper Punch-Marked Coins (PMC). Rising Vaishyas and merchants wanted higher status than Brahmanism gave them → funded non-Vedic faiths; non-violence also ended wars → boosted trade |
| **(d) Appeal to the Laity** | Urbanisation bred inequality and social conflict; common folk wanted relief from violence, theft, hatred → welcomed simple, ascetic, peace-centred message |

> 💡 **Memory tip — "KAVP"**: **K**shatriya reaction, **A**grarian economy, **V**aishya patronage, **P**eople's (laity) appeal

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (Practice Q — Mains):** Discuss the causes for the emergence of new religions in the sixth century BCE — use KAVP framework

## 2. EARLY BUDDHIST LITERATURE

### Two Categories:
- **(a) Canonical** — texts directly linked to the Buddha, laying down basic tenets (e.g. Tipitakas)
- **(b) Non-Canonical / Semi-Canonical** — commentaries, treatises, histories, grammars

### Non-Canonical Texts

| Text | Note |
|------|------|
| **Milindapanho** | Pali; dialogue between Indo-Greek king **Milinda/Menander** and monk **Nagasena** |
| **Nettipakarana** | "The Book of Guidance" — connected account of the Buddha's teachings |
| **Visuddhimagga** | "The Path to Purity" by **Buddhaghosa**; discipline → nibbana |
| **Nidanakatha** | First connected **life story** of the Buddha |
| **Dipavamsa & Mahavamsa** | Pali; history + myth of Buddha, Buddhist councils, Ashoka, arrival in Sri Lanka |
| **Mahavastu** | Mixed Sanskrit–Prakrit; sacred **biography (hagiography)** of the Buddha |

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (UPSC 1997):** Milindapanho is in the form of a dialogue between king Menander and Buddhist monk — **Answer: Nagasena** ✅

### The Tipitaka (Pali) / Tripitaka (Sanskrit) = "Three Baskets"
*(Named because teachings were written on long narrow leaves stored in baskets)*

| Pitaka | Meaning | Contents |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **Sutta Pitaka** | Conventional teaching | Buddha's discourses (dialogue form) — called "Buddha Vacana" (word of the Buddha); has **5 Nikayas** |
| **Vinaya Pitaka** | Disciplinary code | Rules for monks/nuns (Sangha); includes **Patimokka** (transgressions + atonements); plus some Jatakas |
| **Abhidhamma Pitaka** | Moral psychology | Systematic study of Sutta teachings via summaries, Q&A, lists |

> 💡 **Memory tip — "S-V-A"**: Sutta, Vinaya, Abhidhamma

### The 5 Nikayas (of Sutta Pitaka):
1. **Digha Nikaya** — long discourses
2. **Majjhima Nikaya** — medium-length discourses
3. **Samyutta Nikaya** — "kindred sayings"
4. **Anguttara Nikaya** — discourses arranged by number
5. **Khuddaka Nikaya** — smaller collection (15 books) including:
   - **Jatakas** (Buddha's past births)
   - **Dhammapada** (ethical verses)
   - **Theragatha/Therigatha** (songs of elder monks/nuns)
   - Niddesa, Buddhavamsa, Patisambhida

> 📌 Book counts: Vinaya = 5 books, Sutta = 5 collections, Abhidhamma = 7 books

### The 7 Books of Abhidhamma Pitaka:
Dhamma-sangani, Vibhanga, Dhatu-katha, Puggala-pannatti, **Katha-vatthu**, Yamaka, Patthana
> 📌 **Katha-vatthu** was added at the **Third Council** by **Moggaliputta Tissa**

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (UPSC 1996):** Therigatha is NOT part of Jaina literature — **TRUE** ✅ It is part of **Khuddaka Nikaya** of Buddhist Sutta Pitaka — songs of elder Buddhist **nuns**

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (UPSC 1998):** The concept of Eight-fold path forms the theme of — **Answer: Dharma-Chakra Pravartna Sutta** ✅

## 3. LIFE OF THE BUDDHA

| Fact | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Names** | Siddhartha → Buddha; also **Sakyamuni / Tathagata** |
| **Born** | **563 BCE**, Vaishakha Poornima, at **Lumbini** (near Kapilavastu, Nepal) |
| **Clan/Father** | Republican **Sakya clan**; father **Suddhodana** (chief of Kapilavastu, Kosala kingdom) |
| **Mother** | **Mahamaya** (died 7 days after birth); raised by step-mother **Gautami** |
| **Prophecy** | Brahmanas saw **32 marks of a Mahapurusha** → either world conqueror or world renouncer |
| **Marriage** | At 16 to **Yashodhara**; son **Rahula** |
| **Four Sights** | Old man, sick man, corpse, ascetic → triggered renunciation |
| **Great Renunciation** | At **29** — left with charioteer **Channa** and horse **Kanthaka** (*Mahabhinishkramana*) |
| **Teachers** | **Alara Kalama**, then **Uddaka Ramaputta** — unconvinced by both |
| **5 Ascetic Companions** | Kondanna, Bhaddiya, Vappa, Mahanama, Assaji |
| **Pre-enlightenment** | Milk-rice from village girl **Sujata**; kusa grass mat; sat under **Peepal tree** facing east; defeated **Mara** (Lord of Illusions) via earth-touching gesture |
| **Enlightenment** | At **35**, at **Bodh Gaya/Uruvela** (Magadha, Bihar), on **Niranjana river** → the **Bodhi Tree** |
| **First Sermon** | At **Sarnath** deer park, to his 5 companions = **Dhammachakka-Pavattana** ("turning the wheel of dharma"); they became Arhats |
| **Death (Parinibbana)** | At **80**, at **Kusinara** (in the Mallas) |

> 💡 **Memory tip — Ages**: Renounced at **29**, Enlightened at **35**, Died at **80**

> 📌 **Bodhi Tree note**: Ashoka's queen **Tissarakkha** had it killed; later cut by **Pushyamitra Shunga** (2nd c. BCE) and **Shashanka** (600 CE) — each time replanted; still revered at Bodh Gaya

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (UPSC 2005):** Which was NOT a contemporary of the other three — Bimbisara, Gautam Buddha, **Milinda**, Prasenjit? — **Answer: Milinda** ✅ (Milinda/Menander was a Greek king of 2nd century BCE — much later)

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (UPSC 2015 & 2014):** Kingdoms associated with life of Buddha — **Answer: Avanti, Kosala and Magadha** ✅ (NOT Gandhara)

## 4. DOCTRINES OF BUDDHISM

### The Three Jewels (Triratna)
1. **Buddha** — Founder/Teacher
2. **Dhamma** — Teachings
3. **Sangha** — Order of monks/nuns (torch-bearers of Dhamma)

> 💡 **Memory tip — "BDS"**: Buddha, Dhamma, Sangha

### Four Noble Truths (Ariya-Sachchani)

| # | Sanskrit/Pali | Meaning |
|---|--------------|---------|
| 1 | **Dukkha** | Life has suffering (*sabbam dukkham*) — actual + potential pain |
| 2 | **Samudaya** | There is a cause — **desire (Trishna)** is the root of suffering |
| 3 | **Nirodha** | Suffering can end — by attaining Nirvana |
| 4 | **Astangika-Marga** | There is a **path** to end suffering (the Eightfold Path) |

### The Eightfold Path (Astangika-Marga)
*("Right" = proper/whole/complete, not opposite of "wrong"; about "unlearning")*

1. **Right Vision** (*Samma-Ditthi*) — correct understanding of reality
2. **Right Thought** (*Samma-Sankappa*) — love and compassion
3. **Right Speech** (*Samma-Vaca*) — truthful, non-harmful
4. **Right Action** (*Samma-Kammanta*) — ethical, non-exploitative (→ Five Precepts)
5. **Right Livelihood** (*Samma-Ajiva*) — non-exploitative living
6. **Right Effort** (*Samma-Vayama*) — directing energy to creative/healing action
7. **Right Mindfulness** (*Samma-Sati*) — self-awareness
8. **Right Concentration** (*Samma-Samadhi*) — meditation → Buddhahood

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (UPSC 1998):** Eight-fold path forms theme of — **Answer: Dharma-Chakra Pravartna Sutta** ✅

### The Five Precepts (under Right Action — for monks AND laity)
1. Do not commit violence (no killing of any being)
2. Do not covet property of others
3. Avoid sensual misconduct (laity: chastity)
4. Do not lie (no slander/deceit)
5. Do not use intoxicants

> 📌 **Three extra precepts for monks/nuns**: no eating after midday; no dancing/music/perfumes/ornaments; no luxurious beds or handling gold/silver (money)

## 5. NIRVANA / NIBBANA

- **Etymology**: Pali *Nibbana* = "Ni" (not) + "Vana" (craving) → **extinction of desire/craving**
- NOT physical death — the dying out of desire, attachment, greed, ignorance, hatred and ego
- **Mahaparinirvana** = nirvana attained on death
- Buddhism accepts transmigration and impermanence, but **rejects God and soul (atman)**
- The soul is a "myth" in Buddhism

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (UPSC 2013):** Which best describes Nirvana in Buddhism? — **Answer: The extinction of the flame of desire** ✅ (NOT annihilation of self, NOT a state of bliss, NOT beyond comprehension)

## 6. CORE PHILOSOPHICAL DOCTRINES

### A. Pratityasamutpada (Dependent Origination)
- Central Buddhist law of causation: **"this being, that arises"**
- Nothing exists independently; all things arise from causes and conditions
- Expressed as **chain of 12 links (Nidanas)**:

| # | Nidana | Meaning |
|---|--------|---------|
| 1 | **Avidya** | Ignorance |
| 2 | **Samskara** | Karmic formations |
| 3 | **Vijnana** | Consciousness |
| 4 | **Namarupa** | Name and form |
| 5 | **Shadayatana** | Six sense bases |
| 6 | **Sparsha** | Contact |
| 7 | **Vedana** | Feeling |
| 8 | **Trishna** | Craving |
| 9 | **Upadana** | Clinging |
| 10 | **Bhava** | Becoming |
| 11 | **Jati** | Birth |
| 12 | **Jaramarana** | Old age and death |

> 💡 Breaking this chain (removing ignorance) ends the cycle of rebirth → Nirvana

### B. Three Marks of Existence (Trilakshana / Tilakkhana)
1. **Anicca** — impermanence (everything is transient)
2. **Dukkha** — suffering/unsatisfactoriness
3. **Anatta (Anatman)** — no permanent self/soul

> 💡 **Memory tip — "A-D-A"**: Anicca, Dukkha, Anatta

### C. Kshanikavada
- Doctrine of **momentariness** — everything exists only for a moment, in constant flux

### D. Anatmavada / Nairatmyavada
- **Denial of a permanent soul** — key point separating Buddhism from Upanishads
- Rebirth happens via a **stream of causation (Santana)**, not a transmigrating soul

### E. Karma in Buddhism
- **Intention (Cetana)** is what makes karma — *"It is intention that I call karma"* — said Buddha
- Unlike Jainism where even **unintentional** harm creates karma
- Karma = mental/intentional force (NOT physical particles as in Jainism)

## 9. BUDDHIST COSMOLOGY — TEN REALMS OF BEING

**Higher Realms:**
1. **Buddha** — Fully enlightened; attained Nirvana; guides others
2. **Bodhisattva** — Seeks enlightenment but delays to help all beings
3. **Pratyeka Buddha** — Self-enlightened but does NOT teach others
4. **Sravaka** — Disciple who attains enlightenment by following Buddha's teachings

**Middle Realms:**
5. **Heavenly beings (Devas)** — Live in pleasure due to good karma but NOT liberated
6. **Humans** — Experience both joy and suffering; **best state for attaining Nirvana**
7. **Asura** — Powerful but jealous beings driven by ego and conflict

**Lower Realms:**
8. **Beasts (Animals)** — Live in ignorance and instinct
9. **Preta (Hungry ghosts)** — Tormented by endless desire
10. **Hell beings** — Undergo intense suffering due to negative karma

> 📌 Man sits at midpoint — can sink to hell or rise to Buddhahood
> 📌 Non-violence (Ahimsa) stressed, but **vegetarianism is NOT required** — monks could eat meat

## 10. BUDDHISM AND WOMEN

- Initially Buddha was **reluctant** to admit women, fearing they'd corrupt the Sangha
- Relented after **Ananda** interceded for **Maha Prajapati Gautami** (his step-mother) — first woman ordained
- Admission conditional on **eight rules** subordinating nuns to monks:
  - A nun, however senior, **salutes any monk**
  - Nuns can't admonish monks (but monks can admonish nuns)
  - Confession at joint meetings; two-year probation
- Famous Bhikkhunis: **Dhammadinna, Sumangala, Mathika, Vasanti, Sumedha, Khema, Utpalavarna**
- Former courtesans gained respect: **Amrapali** (Vaishali), **Padmavati** (Ujjain), **Arthakasi** (Kasi)

> 📌 Buddha reportedly said admitting women would shorten the faith from **1000 → 500 years**

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (Practice Q):** Women were NOT admitted in Buddhist Sanghas — **FALSE** ❌ Women WERE admitted as bhikkhunis

## 11. BUDDHISM VS BRAHMANISM

- Buddhism judged **varna by action, not birth**
- In Anguttara Nikaya, one who joins the Sangha becomes **"vevanniyanti"** (without varna)
- Sangha drew from ALL castes:
  - **Upali** (barber), **Chunda** (blacksmith)
  - Brahmana monks: Mahakassapa, Sariputta, Mahamoggallana
  - Kshatriya monks: the Buddha himself, Ananda, Aniruddha
- **Mahakassapa** (a Brahmana) presided over the **First Council**
- But Buddhism did NOT abolish class — barred **debtors, slaves, soldiers** (without leave)

## 12. SPREAD AND POPULARITY OF BUDDHISM

### Why it Spread:
- More **liberal and democratic** — attacked varna, admitted all castes and women
- **Buddha's personality** + reasoned doctrine (wit, calm, logic)
- Teachings in **Pali** (people's language), NOT Sanskrit
- **Royal patronage** — Magadha, Kosala, Kaushambi, Gana-Sanghas
- Practical solutions to social/economic ills of new urban economy

### Ashoka's Missionaries (High-Yield MCQ)

| Missionary | Destination |
|-----------|------------|
| **Mahendra + Sanghamitra** (son + daughter) | Sri Lanka |
| **Sona + Uttara** | Suvarnabhumi (Myanmar/Thailand) |
| **Mahadev** | Mysore |
| **Dharamarakshit** | Himalayas |
| **Maharakshit** | Land of the Greeks |

### Spread to China:
- **1st c. CE** — Dharamrakshit and Kashyap Matanga went to China; translated Buddhist texts
- **Kumarajiva** went to China in **401 CE** (became head of Buddhist scholars there)
- Chinese pilgrims: **Fa-Hien** and **Huen Tsang** visited India and carried texts back

## 13. DECLINE OF BUDDHISM (from early 12th century CE)

| Cause | Explanation |
|-------|-------------|
| **(a) Internal degeneration** | Succumbed to rituals and dogma it once denounced; monks abandoned Pali for Sanskrit |
| **(b) Corruption** | From 1st c. CE, took up large-scale idol worship + donations → corruption of ascetic life; Mahayana even made Buddha a god |
| **(c) Persecution** | **Pushyamitra Shunga**, Huna king **Mihirakula**, **Shashanka** of Gauda; Turkish invaders sacked rich monasteries → loss of royal patronage |
| **(d) Revival of Brahmanism** | Vaishnavism (Bhakti, direct God-devotee link, patronised by Guptas) drew masses away |

> 💡 **Memory tip — Persecutors "P-M-S"**: Pushyamitra Shunga, Mihirakula (Huna), Shashanka

## 14. BUDDHIST COUNCILS

| Council | Year | Place | Ruler | President | Outcome |
|---------|------|-------|-------|-----------|---------|
| **1st** | 483 BCE | **Rajgriha** (Saptaparni cave) | Ajatashatru | **Mahakassapa** | Teachings split into 3 baskets; Upali recited Vinaya, Ananda recited Sutta |

## 15. THREE SCHOOLS OF BUDDHISM

| Feature | Hinayana (Theravada) | Mahayana | Vajrayana |
|---------|---------------------|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | "Lesser Path"; Theravada = "Doctrine of the Elders" | "Greater Path" | "Vehicle of the Thunderbolt"; also Mantrayana/Tantrayana |
| **Nature** | Orthodox; truer to Buddha; **original school** | Heterodox; later development | Esoteric extension; appeared E-India 8th c. CE |
| **Language** | Pali | Sanskrit | Sanskrit + symbolic "Twilight Language" (*samdhya-bhasa*) |
| **View of Buddha** | Buddha = **human**; no idol worship | Buddha = **god**; idol worship | Quick path via magical power (Vajra) |
| **Goal** | Individual salvation; aim = **Nirvana** | Universal liberation; aim = spiritual upliftment | Mantras, mandalas, central guru/lama |
| **Ideal** | **Arhat** (individual liberation) | **Bodhisattva** (universal liberation) | — |
| **Today** | Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Thailand, SE Asia | Japan, China, E-Asia | Tibet, Nepal, Bhutan, Mongolia |
| **Royal Patron** | **Ashoka** | **Kanishka**, later Harsha | — |

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (UPSC 2016):** Bodhisattva concept is central to Hinayana — **FALSE** ❌ Central to **Mahayana**. Bodhisattva delays nirvana to help all beings ✅

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (UPSC 2020):** Sthaviravadins belong to Mahayana Buddhism — **FALSE** ❌ They belong to **Hinayana** Buddhism ✅

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (Practice Q):** Mahayana Buddhism believes in idol worship while Hinayana does not — **TRUE** ✅

### Mahayana Schools:

| School | Founder | Doctrine |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **Madhyamika / Sunyavada** | **Nagarjuna** | Doctrine of **Sunyata** (emptiness): reality is void of independent existence |
| **Yogachara / Vijnanavada** | **Asanga & Vasubandhu** | "Consciousness-only"/idealism: only mind/consciousness (vijnana) is ultimately real |

### Trikaya Doctrine (Three Bodies of the Buddha):
1. **Dharmakaya** — the body of truth/essence (ultimate reality)
2. **Sambhogakaya** — the body of bliss/enjoyment (celestial form)
3. **Nirmanakaya** — the body of manifestation (the historical Buddha)

> 💡 **Memory tip — "D-S-N"**: Dharmakaya, Sambhogakaya, Nirmanakaya

## 18. STUPAS AND ARCHITECTURE

### What is a Stupa?
- Hemispherical burial mounds holding relics/ashes; pilgrimage and meditation sites
- Common since Vedic times but raised to cult worship under **Ashoka**
- **9 original stupas** after Buddha's death (8 over relics + 1 over relic-vessel)

### Parts of a Stupa (top to bottom):
**Chhatra** (umbrella) → **Harmika** (square railing) → **Anda** (hemispherical mound) → **Medhi** (cylindrical drum) → **Torana** (entrance gate)

> 💡 **Memory tip — "CHAD"**: Chhatra, Harmika, Anda, Drum

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (UPSC 2013):** Chaitya vs Vihara — **Chaitya = place of worship** (prayer hall with stupa inside); **Vihara = dwelling place of monks** (monastery) ✅

### Types of Stupas:

| Type | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| **Relic Stupa** | Holds relics/remains of Buddha or disciples |
| **Object Stupa** | Holds objects (begging bowl, robe) or scriptures |
| **Commemorative Stupa** | Marks events in lives of Buddha or disciples |

# PART B — JAINISM

## 20. EARLY JAIN LITERATURE

### Preservation of Teachings
- Initially preserved as **oral tradition** — monks strictly followed the **five great vows** of Jainism
- Religious scriptures were **never documented** in early times — because even religious scriptures were considered **possessions**, and monks were bound by the vow of **Non-Possession (Aparigraha)**; hence knowledge could only be preserved orally
- Later, Jaina teachers realised it was extremely difficult to keep memorising the entire Jain literature
- The Jaina holy writings are documented as **"canon"** — however this word is considered **increasingly inadequate** as it implies an unchanging body of texts sanctioned by a central authority
  - **What is Canon?** = a fixed, authoritative collection of sacred texts officially accepted/sanctioned by a religious authority as genuine and binding (e.g. Bible for Christians)
  - The Jainas instead refer to their sacred texts as **Siddhanta or Agama** — denoting **fluid groupings open to change**; not a rigid, fixed collection
- **Ardh-Magadhi** — Eastern dialect of Prakrit — was the language of the **earliest Jaina canonical texts**; particularly associated with the **Shvetambara** tradition. Digambaras consider **Shauraseni Prakrit** as their sacred language

### Compilation Crisis
- Entire canon traditionally compiled in **5th–6th century CE** at a council presided by **Devarddhi Kshamashramana** at **Vallabhi, Gujarat**
- Significant knowledge was already lost; remaining was **polluted with modifications and errors**
- Compilations like **Acharanga Sutra, Sutrakritanga, Kalpasutra** → regarded as early texts

## 22. GANADHARAS AND SRUT-KEVLIS
- **Ganadharas** = immediate disciples of Lord Mahavira (mendicants, possessed perfect knowledge — *keval-gyan*)
- They orally compiled Mahavira's direct preachings into **12 main texts (sutras)** = Angas
- **Srut-kevlis** = monks with knowledge of minimum 10 Purvas ("previous texts" — original teachings of Jinas)
- Srut-kevlis wrote many texts (sutras) expanding subject matter defined in the Angas = called **Angbahyas** (outside of Angas)

### What are Purvas?
- The word literally means **'early'** or **'previous'**
- They are considered the **original teachings of the Jinas (Tirthankaras)** — predating even the Angas
- There were **14 Purvas** in total
- Knowledge of minimum **10 Purvas** was required to be called a Srut-kevli
- The Ganadharas compiled the Angas **to replace the Purvas** — as the Purvas were getting lost
- Eventually **all 14 Purvas were lost** — this is why the 1st Jaina Council at Patliputra (310 BCE) compiled **12 Angas to replace Purvas**
- **Digambaras** reject this replacement — they believe the original Purvas are lost forever and cannot be replaced

> 💡 **Memory tip**: Think of Purvas as the **original Constitution** and Angas as the **amended version** made when the original was getting destroyed

### Key Difference: Ganadharas (Keval-gyan) vs Srut-kevlis

| | Ganadharas (Keval-gyan) | Srut-kevlis |
|--|------------------------|-------------|
| **Type of knowledge** | Perfect/complete — omniscience | Partial — knowledge of minimum 10 Purvas |
| **Source of knowledge** | Direct from Mahavira | Through Purvas (previous texts) |
| **What they did** | Compiled 12 Angas (main texts) | Wrote Angbahyas (texts outside Angas) |
| **Level** | Highest — direct disciples | Lower — knew only previous texts |

> 💡 **Memory tip**: Keval-gyan = **complete omniscience** (like a full cup); Srut-kevlis = **partial knowledge** through texts (like half a cup)

## 26. DETAILED DOCTRINES

### A. ANEKANTAVADA
- Literally: "non-one-sidedness" / "manifoldness" / **non-absolutism**
- Opposed to **ekanta** (one-sidedness or solitary attribute)
- Core principle: encourages acceptance of **relativism and pluralism**
- *"Truth and reality are perceived differently from different points of view; no single point of view is the complete truth"*
- Illustrated by **Adhgajanyayah** — the **"blind men and elephant" parable**:
  - One man felt the trunk, another felt the ears, third felt the tail
  - All claimed to explain the true appearance — but could only partly succeed due to narrow perspectives
- Only **Kevalins** (omniscient beings) can comprehend objects in all aspects; all others capable of only partial knowledge
- Jaina doctrine: objects have infinite modes of existence and qualities → cannot be completely grasped by finite human perception

### B. SYADAVADA
- Doctrine of **'may be'** — emphasises **relativity of all knowledge**
- All judgments are conditional — hold good only in certain conditions, circumstances, or senses
- What is possible = only a number of **partially true statements** about any reality
- Every statement about reality should be prefixed with **"syat"** (meaning 'may be')
- Focus = **language and propositions** — about **how we make claims** about reality
- Example: "This pot *may be* red" — acknowledging the statement is only partially true

### C. NAYAVADA
- System of describing reality **from different points of view**
- *'Naya'* = partially true statements; cannot claim absolute validity
- *'Naya'* can also be defined as a particular opinion framed with a viewpoint that **does not rule out other different viewpoints**
- Expressive of a **partial truth** about an object, as entertained by a knowing agent
- Focus = **perspectives and standpoints** — about **how we look at/perceive** reality from different angles
- Example: Looking at a pot from one angle gives only a partial view

> 📌 **Key Difference between Syadavada and Nayavada:**

| | Syadavada | Nayavada |
|--|-----------|----------|
| **About** | Statements/claims about reality | Viewpoints/standpoints of reality |
| **Tool** | "Syat" prefix on every statement | Multiple *nayas* (partial perspectives) |
| **Question it answers** | How should we **say** things about reality? | How should we **look at** reality? |
| **Level** | Language level | Thought/perception level |

> 💡 **Memory tip**: **Nayavada** = the **thought process** (how you perceive); **Syadavada** = the **expression** of that thought (how you articulate it)

## 27. KARMA AND SALVATION

- Bad deeds of a person **get attached to the soul**
- **Bondage of Karma** = responsible for cycle of life and death
- To attain **Nirvana/Salvation**:
  - Inflow of Karma towards soul must be **stopped**
  - Already accumulated Karmas must be **destroyed** to liberate the soul
- Liberation achieved through **Triratna** (three jewels of Jaina ethics):

### Triratna — Three Jewels

| Jewel | Sanskrit | Meaning |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **Right Faith** | *Samyag Darshana* | Seeing/hearing/feeling things properly; avoiding preconceptions and superstitions that obstruct clear seeing |
| **Right Knowledge** | *Samyag Jnana* | Accurate and sufficient knowledge of the real universe — requires knowledge of five (or six) substances and nine truths; must have right mental attitude |
| **Right Conduct** | *Samyag Charitra* | Living one's life according to Jaina ethical rules; avoiding harm to living things; freeing oneself from attachment and other impure attitudes and thoughts |

## 28. PANCHA MAHAVRATA — Five Great Vows

### i. Non-Violence (AHIMSA) — Cardinal Principle
- **"Ahimsa parmo dharma"** = Non-violence is the supreme religion
- **Cardinal principle** of Jainism — the cornerstone
- All living beings, irrespective of size, shape, or different spiritual developments, are **equal and have souls**
- No living being has the right to harm, injure or kill any other living being — including gods, animals, insects and plants
- *Jains believe even non-living things like air and water have souls (Footnote 14)*

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (UPSC 2023):** 'Souls are not only the property of animal and plant life, but also of rocks, running water and many other natural objects not looked on as living by other religious sects.' — This reflects core belief of **Jainism** ✅ (NOT Buddhism, Shaivism or Vaishnavism)

**Four forms of existence recognised by Jainism:**
1. Gods (*deva*)
2. Humans (*manushya*)
3. Hell beings (*naraki*)
4. Animals and plants (*tiryancha*)

**Further sub-categories — Ekendriya and Nigodas:**
- **Ekendriya** = single-sense bodies — they have only the sense of touch
- Animals and plants (*tiryancha*) are further divided on the basis of the faculty of sense into smaller sub-categories:
  - **Ekendriya** (single-sense bodies — sense of only touch)
  - **Nigodas** = lowest of ekendriya, born in clusters, lives last only a fraction of a second

**Five types of Sthavara (element bodies) — all Ekendriya:**
1. **Earth bodies** (*prithvi*) — e.g. soil, rocks
2. **Water bodies** (*jal*) — e.g. rivers, rain
3. **Fire bodies** (*agni*) — e.g. flame
4. **Air bodies** (*vayu*) — e.g. wind
5. **Vegetation/Plant bodies** (*vanaspati*) — e.g. trees, plants

> 📌 This is why Jains believe even non-living things like air, water and rocks have souls — they are classified as Ekendriya living beings

**Full Hierarchy of Living Beings in Jainism (lowest to highest):**

| Level | Name | Senses | Examples |
|-------|------|--------|----------|
| Lowest | **Nigodas** | Touch only | Born in clusters, life lasts fraction of a second |
| ↑ | **Sthavara** (element bodies) | Touch only | Earth, Water, Fire, Air, Vegetation |
| ↑ | **Two-sensed** (*dvindriya*) | Touch + Taste | Worms, leeches, shells |
| ↑ | **Three-sensed** (*trindriya*) | Touch + Taste + Smell | Ants, lice, bugs |
| ↑ | **Four-sensed** (*chaturindriya*) | Touch + Taste + Smell + Sight | Flies, mosquitoes, butterflies |
| Highest | **Five-sensed** (*panchendriya*) | All five senses | Humans, animals, gods, hell beings |

> 💡 **Memory tip:** Higher the number of senses → higher the life form → more strictly protected under Ahimsa
> - Monks = must avoid harming ALL levels including Nigodas and Sthavara
> - Laypeople = must avoid harming beings with **two or more senses**

**Key Points on Violence:**
- Violence is NOT defined by actual harm — it is the **intention to harm** that makes an action violent
- Absence of compassion, unawareness and ignorance = violence
- Non-violence to be observed in **action, speech and thought**
- One should not be violent, ask others to do so, or **approve** of such activity
- Eventually Jainas prohibited the practice of **war and even agriculture**
- Confined themselves to **trade and mercantile activities**
- Jainism preaches **strict vegetarianism**; prohibits non-vegetarian food

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (UPSC 2000):** Assertion: The emphasis of Jainism on non-violence prevented agriculturists from embracing Jainism. Reason: Cultivation involved killing of insects and pests. — **Answer: Both A and R are true and R explains A** ✅

**Monks vs Laypeople:**
- Monks/renunciants = must refrain from harming even ekendriyas
- General laity = supposed to avoid harming beings with **two or more senses**

### ii. Truth (SATYA)
- Jainism insists one should not only refrain from falsehood but **speak truth which is wholesome and pleasant**
- Must remain **silent** if the truth causes pain, hurt, anger or death of any living being
- Anger, greed, fear, jokes = **breeding grounds of untruth**
- To speak truth requires **moral courage**
- Only those who have conquered greed, fear, anger, jealousy, ego and frivolity can speak the truth
- Truth to be observed in **speech, mind and deed**

### iii. Non-Stealing (ACHAURYA / ASTEYA)
- Stealing = taking another's property without consent, or by **unjust or immoral methods**
- Includes things **lying unattended or unclaimed**
- One should not touch even a worthless thing which does not belong to him
- When accepting alms/help/aid — should not take **more than minimum requirement**
- Taking more than one's need = also considered theft in Jainism

### iv. Celibacy/Chastity (BRAHMACHARYA) — Added by Mahavira
- Total abstinence from **sensual pleasure and pleasure of all five senses**
- Sensual pleasure = infatuating force that sets aside all virtues and reason
- This vow is very difficult to observe in its subtle form
- Even thinking of sensual pleasures (while refraining from physical indulgence) = prohibited in Jainism
- **Monks**: must observe this vow completely
- **Householders**: should not indulge in any physical relationship other than with one's own spouse, and that too of limited nature

### v. Non-Attachment/Non-Possession (APARIGRAHA)
- Attachment to worldly objects = results in bondage to cycle of birth and death
- One who desires spiritual liberation should **withdraw from all attachments** to objects that please any of the five senses
- The more worldly wealth a person possesses → more likely to commit sin to acquire and maintain it → long run he may be unhappy
- Worldly wealth creates attachment → continuously results in greed
- *Mahavira has said: "wants and desires have no end, and only the sky is the limit for them"*

## 30. BUDDHISM AND JAINISM — SIMILARITIES AND DIFFERENCES

### Similarities
- Both **rejected the authority of Vedas**
- Both emphasised **renunciation and human effort** as means of obtaining salvation
- Both established **monastic order for both men and women**
- Both are **fundamentally atheistic** — though they recognise existence of gods, refuse to give them importance in universal scheme
- Both place **Gods lower than Jina/Buddha** (the conqueror)

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (Practice Q):** Jainas believed in the existence of god but placed it below their Jina — **TRUE** ✅. Buddhists believe in the concept of god but do not believe in atman (soul) — **FALSE** ❌ Buddhists believe in **anatman** (no soul) AND also do not give importance to God
- Both Jaina and Buddhist texts criticise Brahmanas, their practice of **animal sacrifices** and ritual authority
- Both focus on **superiority of Kshatriya varna** over all other varnas including Brahmanas
- Both gave a new connotation to the meaning of **'Brahmana'** — shifted emphasis from ascribed status to **good deeds**; used 'Brahmana' in the sense of a wise person with true knowledge living an exemplary life
- Like Buddhism, **people of all castes and social backgrounds** were welcomed in Jainism
- Jainism opened its doors to **women mendicants** (addressed as *aryika* or *sadhvi*)

> - Indifference to the authority of the Vedas → **Common** ✅
> - Denial of efficacy of rituals → **Common** ✅
> - Non-injury to animal life → **Common** ✅
> - **Answer: ii, iii and iv (NOT i)** — Middle Path trap is the most common mistake

### Notable Jain Monks from Non-Brahmin Backgrounds

## 31. SPREAD AND INFLUENCE OF JAINISM

### Geographic Spread
- Gradually spread to **western India** where Brahmanical religion was weak
- Early Jainas adopted **Prakrit language** of the common people (discarded Sanskrit which was patronised by Brahmanas)
- This greatly helped in **widening the follower base** of Jainism

### Royal Patronage
- **Chandragupta Maurya** — himself became a Jain ascetic and spent his last years in Karnataka
- Great **famine in Magadha** (200 years after Mahavira's death) contributed to spread in southern India
  - Famine lasted about **12 years**
  - Many Jaina followers under leadership of **Bhadrabahu** (later known as *Digambaras*) went South
- Around **4th century BCE** — spread to Kalinga, greatly popularised by King **Kharavela**
- In about **2nd and 1st centuries BCE** — reached Malwa, Gujarat, and Rajasthan
- Later penetrated southern districts of Tamil Nadu

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (UPSC 2004):** Jainism enjoyed the patronage of the Kalinga king Kharvela in the first century BCE — **TRUE** ✅

### Jainism vs Buddhism — Spread
- Jainism did not win as much **state patronage** as Buddhism
- Was not as fast to spread as Buddhism in early times
- **Still retains its hold in many areas** of Indian sub-continent
- Buddhism has **practically disappeared** from the land of its birth

## 32. DIFFERENT SCHOOLS OF JAINISM

### Major Sects: Digambara vs Shvetambara

| Point | Digambara | Shvetambara |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **(a) Name/Nudity** | "Sky-clad" — stress practice of nudity as absolute pre-requisite to mendicant's path and attainment of salvation | "White-clad" — practice of complete nudity is NOT essential to attain liberation |
| **(b) Magadha Famine** | Under leadership of **Bhadrabahu**, migrated South and lived in old traditional way | Under leadership of **Sthalabahu**, stayed back at Magadha; accused returning Jainas of violating rules by wearing white clothing |
| **(c) Liberation of Women** | Women lack the body and rigid will to attain *moksha* — must be reborn as a man first | Women capable of same spiritual accomplishments as men; 19th Tirthankara = female named **Malli** (only female Tirthankara) |
| **(d) Omniscient's Food** | On attaining enlightenment, an omniscient is freed from ordinary needs (hunger, thirst, sleep, disease, fear) | Even the omniscient requires food |
| **(e) Hagiography of Mahavira** | Rubbishes the Shvetambara claim about embryo episode as unreliable and absurd | Mahavira was born of a Kshatriya lady Trishala — though conception took place in womb of Brahman lady Devananda; embryo change affected by God Indra on 83rd day after conception |
| **(f) Mahavira's Marriage** | Mahavira **never married**; renounced world while parents were alive | Mahavira married Princess Yashoda at young age; had daughter Priyadarshana; led householder's life till thirty |
| **(g) Idols of Tirthankaras** | Depicted as **nude, unadorned, downcast eyes** in contemplative mood | Depicted wearing **loin-cloth, bedecked with jewels, glass eyes** inserted in marble |
| **(h) Canonical Literature** | Original and genuine texts were **lost long ago**; refuse to accept first council under Acharya Sthulabhadra | Believe in **validity and sacredness** of canonical literature — 12 angas and sutras as they exist now |
| **(i) Terminology** | Use term **'Purana'** for hagiographies | Use term **'Charita'** |
| **(j) Food of Ascetics** | Take food **standing**, with knotted upturned palms, in **one house only** where their *sankalpa* (preconceived idea) is fulfilled | Collect food from **different houses** |
| **(k) Possessions of Ascetics** | Must renounce ALL possessions including clothes; allowed only 2: **Rajoharana** (peacock-feather broom) and **Kamandalu** (wooden water-pot for toilet hygiene) | Allowed **14 possessions** including loin-cloth, shoulder-cloth, etc. |

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (UPSC 2004):** The Jainas who remained under the leadership of Bhadrabahu were called Shvetambaras — **FALSE** ❌ Those who followed Bhadrabahu and migrated South became **Digambaras**. Those who stayed back under Sthalabahu became **Shvetambaras**. Classic trap question!

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (Practice Q):** Shvetambaras are naked while Digambaras are white clad — **FALSE** ❌ It is the **reverse** — Digambaras = naked (sky-clad); Shvetambaras = white clad

## 33. DIGAMBARA SUB-SECTS

### Major Sub-Sects

#### Who are Bhattarakas?
- **Bhattaraka** literally means **"venerable lord"** or **"respected one"**
- They are **heads of Jaina Mathas (monasteries)** — unique institution in Digambara Jainism
- A middle ground between a **fully initiated naked Digambara monk** and a **layperson**
- Wear **saffron/orange robes** — NOT naked like full Digambara monks
- Role: manage temples, conduct religious ceremonies, settle disputes, guide laypeople
- Highly learned in Jaina scriptures and philosophy
- Over time became very powerful → accused of accumulating wealth (contradicting Aparigraha), conducting elaborate rituals, introducing idol worship of non-Tirthankara deities → triggered **Terapantha revolt**
- **North India** → Bhattaraka institution **lost its importance** due to Terapantha revolt
- **South India** → Bhattarakas **continue to play an important role** even today

> 💡 **Simple analogy:** Think of Bhattarakas like powerful bishops in Christianity — heads of religious institutions who became too powerful, eventually triggering a reform movement

| Sub-Sect | Key Feature | Region |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| **Bisapantha** | Support Dharma-gurus i.e. **Bhattarakas** (heads of Jaina Mathas); worship idols of Tirthankaras AND Ksetrapala, Padmavati and other deities; worship with saffron, flowers, fruits, sweets, incense; sit on ground while worshipping; perform **Arati** (waving of lights over idol); distribute prasada | All Digambara Jainas from Maharashtra, Karnataka, South India, Rajasthan, Gujarat |
| **Terapantha** | Arose as **revolt against domination and conduct of Bhattarakas** → consequently institution of Bhattarakas lost importance in North India (however in South India Bhattarakas continue to play important role); worship idols of Tirthankaras but **NOT** Ksetrapala, Padmavati and other deities; use sacred rice ('Aksata'), cloves, sandal, almonds, dry coconuts, dates; **stand** while worshipping; do **NOT** perform Arati or distribute prasada | More numerous in Uttar Pradesh, Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh |

### Minor Sub-Sects

| Sub-Sect | Key Feature | Region |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| **Taranapantha / Samaiyapantha** | Also called Samaiyapantha; followers worship Sarnaya (sacred books), NOT idols; 3 traits: (i) aversion to idol worship, (ii) absence of outward religious practices, (iii) ban on caste distinctions; strongly refute idolatry but have temples for sacred books | Few in number; Bundelkhand, Malwa area of Madhya Pradesh, Khandesh area of Maharashtra |
| **Gumanapantha** | Stresses purity of conduct and self-discipline; strict adherence to precepts; prohibit lighting of candles or lamps in Jaina temples (violates non-violence principle); only visit/view image — do NOT make offerings | Mostly concentrated in Rajasthan, specifically Jaipur |
| **Totapantha** | Came into existence from differences between Bisapantha (twenty) and Terapantha (thirteen) sub-sects; followers believe in doctrines of both Bisapantha and Terapantha to some extent | Extremely few; found in some pockets in Madhya Pradesh |

## 34. SHVETAMBARA SUB-SECTS

| Sub-Sect | Key Feature | Region |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| **Murtipujaka** | Thorough worshippers of idols; offer flowers, fruits, saffron, etc.; adorn idols with rich clothes and jewelled ornaments; stay in temples or specially reserved buildings (*upasrayas*); 4 attributes: (i) Pujera (worshippers), (ii) Deravasi (temple residents), (iii) Chaityavasi (temple residents), (iv) Mandira-margi (temple goers) | Largely concentrated in Gujarat |

## 36. KEY TERMS / GLOSSARY

| Key Term | Meaning | Etymology / Easy Explanation |
|----------|---------|------------------------------|
| **Basadis** | Jaina monastic establishment | From Kannada — Jaina temples/monasteries in South India; like a 'base' where monks reside |
| **Avadhijnana** | Superhuman cognition | *Avadhi* = limit/boundary + *jnana* = knowledge → knowledge that goes **beyond normal limits**; clairvoyance-like perception |
| **Ganadharas** | Chief disciples of Mahavira | *Gana* = group/community + *dhara* = holder → **"holders of the community"**; direct disciples who memorised and compiled Mahavira's teachings |
| **Siddha** | Fully liberated soul | *Siddh* = accomplished → one who has **accomplished** the ultimate goal of liberation; free from all karma forever |
| **Dravya** | Substance | Same root as Hindi *dravya* (matter/money) → the **basic building blocks** of existence in Jain philosophy; 6 types exist |
| **Guna** | Quality/attribute | Same word used across Indian philosophy → the **inherent qualities** that belong to a substance (e.g. colour is a Guna of matter) |
| **Paryaya** | Mode/modification | *Pari* = around + *aya* = coming → the **changing modes/forms** a substance takes over time (e.g. ice → water → steam are Paryayas of H₂O) |
| **Jiva** | Soul/living being | Root *jiv* = to live → **that which lives**; Jainism believes every living entity has a Jiva; opposite = Ajiva (non-soul) |
| **Eva** | In fact/verily | Sanskrit word meaning **"indeed" or "in fact"** — used in Jain texts to emphasise a truth being stated |
| **Pudgala** | Matter (aggregates of atoms) | *Pud* = to fill + *gala* = to empty → matter that **fills and empties** constantly; has form, colour, taste, smell and can be touched — unlike soul |
| **Chaitanya** | Consciousness | Root *chet* = to be aware → **pure awareness/sentience**; the defining characteristic of a soul (Jiva); what distinguishes living from non-living |
| **Virya** | Energy/vigour | Same as Sanskrit *veerya* (strength) → the **energy or power** of the soul; full Virya = infinite energy of a liberated soul |
| **Mohaniya** | Delusion-causing karmas | *Moha* = delusion/attachment → karmas that cause **delusion and attachment**; most dangerous type of karma as it prevents right faith and right conduct |
| **Asarva** | Inflow (of karma) | *A* = towards + *sarva* = all → the **inflow of karmic particles** into the soul through actions, speech and thought; what binds the soul |
| **Nirjara** | Wearing out (of karma) | *Nir* = out + *jara* = wearing away → the **shedding/wearing out** of accumulated karmas; achieved through penance and austerities |
| **Gunasthanas** | Stages of purification | *Guna* = quality + *sthana* = stage/place → the **14 stages of spiritual development** a soul passes through from ignorance to full liberation |
| **Arhat** | One who has attained kevalajnana | *Arh* = to deserve/be worthy → **"the worthy one"**; has attained omniscience but still lives in the world; same as Jina |
| **Tirthankara** | Ford-maker; Arhat who can teach | *Tirtha* = ford/crossing + *kara* = maker → one who **"makes a ford"** across the ocean of suffering; an Arhat who additionally has the ability to preach and establish the path for others |
| **Salekhana** | Death through fasting | *Sal* = properly + *lekhana* = thinning → **gradually thinning the body** by reducing food until death; considered highest form of death in Jainism; also called *Samadhi Marana* |
| **Divya-dhvani** | Divine sound of the Jina | *Divya* = divine + *dhvani* = sound → the **divine vibration/sound** emanating from a Jina when he reaches omniscience; not a language but understood by all beings |
| **Samavasarana** | Divine assembly hall | *Sama* = equal + *ava* = down + *sarana* = refuge → the **divine preaching assembly** where the Jina's Divya-dhvani is heard; all beings — gods, humans, animals — sit equally and understand it |
| **Keval-gyan** | Perfect/infinite knowledge | *Keval* = alone/absolute + *gyan* = knowledge → **absolute knowledge** not dependent on senses or mind; possessed by Ganadharas; the highest form of knowledge in Jainism |

## 37. AJIVIKAS — Related Heterodox Sect

### Overview
- Buddhism and Jainism were NOT the only religions challenging Brahmanical dominance
- According to Buddhist sources, **more than 62 sects and philosophies** flourished in this period
- **Ajivikas** = "Followers of the way of life" — an ascetic order that started at the time of Buddha and Mahavira; lasted until the **14th century**

### Founder and Key Facts
- Founded by **Makkhali Gosala**
- Very popular in **Magadha in 3rd century BCE**
- Mauryan kings donated several **caves in honour of Ajivika monks**
- For some six years, **Mahavira's hardship was shared by Gosala**, but they ultimately quarrelled; Gosala left Mahavira to found the **Ajivikas**
- Gosala's doctrine = **pre-determinism** or **'niyati'** (destiny)
- His doctrines known only from **Buddhist and Jaina sources**
- He was lowborn and **died after a quarrel with Mahavira** shortly before the Buddha died

### Doctrine of Niyati (Destiny)
- Ajivikas were **rigid fatalists and determinists**
- Held that affairs of the entire universe were ordered by a cosmic force called **niyati** (meaning 'rule' or 'destiny')
- This included an individual's fate, to the **last detail and spiritual destiny**
- **No human effort** could have any effect against niyati
- Therefore *karma* is a **fallacy**
- Nirvana was only reached after living through an immense number of lives — which proceeded automatically, like the unwinding of a ball of thread
- The last life being as an **Ajivika monk**
- Ajivikas practised austerities rather than a static and melancholy view of the human condition
- Some later Ajivikas worshipped **Gosala as a divinity**
- The tenet of *niyati* developed into the doctrine that **all change was illusory and that everything was eternally immobile**

## 38. ADDITIONAL EXAM-RELEVANT POINTS (From General Knowledge)

### Jainism's Contribution to Indian Culture
- Jainism contributed significantly to **Kannada, Tamil, and Hindi literature**
- **Apabhramsha language** — greatly developed by Jaina scholars; bridge between Sanskrit and modern Indian languages
- Jaina scholars preserved and enriched **mathematics** — *Mahaviracharya* wrote *Ganitsarasangraha* (9th century) — first systematic work on arithmetic in Indian mathematics
- Jainism contributed to **democratic ethos** through its open-door policy for all castes

### Jain Art and Architecture
- **Dilwara Temples** (Mount Abu, Rajasthan) — finest examples of Jain architecture (11th–13th century CE); famous for intricate marble carvings
- **Gomateshwara statue** (Shravanabelagola, Karnataka) — 57 feet tall monolithic statue of Bahubali; built in 10th century by Chamundaraya
- **Ellora Caves** — Jain caves (Caves 30–34) are among the magnificent examples
- **Khajuraho** — Jain temples built by Chandela rulers

### Jainism and Politics
- Chandragupta Maurya — embraced Jainism, abdicated throne, went to Shravanabelagola with Bhadrabahu, and died by **Salekhana**
- **Amoghavarsha** (Rashtrakuta ruler) — was a great patron of Jainism
- Jain traders and merchants played significant role in the **economic history of Deccan and South India**

### Jainism and Modern India
- Jains constitute approximately **0.4% of India's population** but contribute disproportionately to trade, commerce and philanthropy
- **Paryushana** = most important Jain festival — period of intense spiritual practice (8 days for Shvetambaras, 10 days for Digambaras)
- **Mahavir Jayanti** = birth anniversary of Mahavira; national holiday in India

## 43. BUDDHISM vs JAINISM — DETAILED COMPARISON

### A. Similarities

| Point | Both Buddhism and Jainism |
|-------|--------------------------|
| **Origin** | Both founded by **Kshatriyas** in 6th century BCE |
| **Vedas** | Both **rejected the authority of Vedas** |
| **Rituals** | Both **denied the efficacy of rituals** and animal sacrifices |
| **Non-violence** | Both stressed **Ahimsa** (non-injury to animal life) |
| **Caste** | Both **rejected caste by birth**; welcomed all social backgrounds |
| **Renunciation** | Both emphasised **renunciation and human effort** for salvation |
| **Monasticism** | Both established **monastic orders for men AND women** |
| **Brahmanas** | Both texts **criticise Brahmanas** and their ritual authority |
| **Kshatriya superiority** | Both placed **Kshatriya varna above Brahmana** |
| **'Brahmana' redefined** | Both redefined Brahmana as one who lives by **good deeds**, not birth |
| **God** | Both **fundamentally atheistic** — recognise gods but give them no real importance |
| **Karma** | Both believe in **Karma and rebirth** |
| **Pali/Prakrit** | Both used **people's languages** (NOT Sanskrit) |

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (UPSC 2012 & 1996):** What was common to BOTH — **Avoidance of extremities = NOT common** ❌ (Middle Path = Buddhism only); **Rejection of Vedas, Denial of rituals, Non-injury to animals = ALL common** ✅

### B. Differences

| Point | Buddhism | Jainism |
|-------|---------|---------|
| **Soul** | **No soul (Anatman)** — soul is a myth | **Soul (Jiva) exists** in every being including rocks, water |
| **Karma** | **Mental/intentional** force; intention (Cetana) is key | **Physical particles** that literally stick to the soul |
| **Unintentional harm** | Less karma — intention matters | Even **unintentional harm creates karma** |
| **Austerity** | **Middle Path** — avoid extremes of penance AND pleasure | **Severe austerities** — Jainism embraces extreme penance |
| **Non-violence** | Ahimsa important; **vegetarianism NOT compulsory** for all monks | Ahimsa is cardinal; **strict vegetarianism** compulsory |
| **Agriculture** | Allowed | **Prohibited** (farming kills insects = Ahimsa violation) |
| **Trade** | Vaishya patrons; no specific restriction | Jainas confined themselves specifically to **trade/mercantile** |
| **Liberation** | Extinguishing desire = **Nirvana** | Shedding all karmic particles = **Moksha** |
| **Transmigration** | **Consciousness stream (Santana)** carries forward; no soul | **Soul (Jiva)** transmigrates carrying karmic particles |
| **God** | Rejects God; **silent on creation** | Rejects God; world created/maintained by **Universal Law** |
| **Spread** | Spread worldwide; **disappeared from India** | Spread limited; **still survives in India** |
| **Language of texts** | **Pali** (canonical) | **Ardh-Magadhi Prakrit** (Shvetambara) / Shauraseni (Digambara) |
| **Councils** | 4 major councils | 2 major councils |
| **Women** | Admitted with restrictions (8 rules) | Admitted; addressed as *aryika* or *sadhvi* |

## 44. TEXTS COMPARISON — BUDDHISM vs JAINISM

| Aspect | Buddhism | Jainism |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **Sacred Canon** | **Tipitaka** (Three Baskets) — Sutta, Vinaya, Abhidhamma | **Siddhanta/Agama** — 12 Angas, 12 Upangas, etc. |
| **Language** | Pali (Hinayana); Sanskrit (Mahayana) | Ardh-Magadhi (Shvetambara); Shauraseni (Digambara) |
| **Original texts** | Preserved and still accepted | **Lost** according to Digambaras |
| **Hagiography term** | — | **Purana** (Digambara) / **Charita** (Shvetambara) |
| **Life stories** | Jatakas (past births of Buddha) | Puranas/Charitas (life stories of Tirthankaras) |
| **Canonical split** | Hinayana canon (Pali) vs Mahayana canon (Sanskrit) | Shvetambara canon (12 Angas + others) vs Digambara (Siddhanta) |
| **Key doctrinal text** | **Milindapanho** (doctrine via dialogue) | **Tattvartha Sutra** (main doctrines of both sects) |
| **Non-canonical gems** | Dipavamsa, Mahavamsa, Buddhacharita | Adi Purana, Trishashtilakshana Mahapurana, Parishishtaparvan |
| **Ethical verses** | **Dhammapada** | — |
| **Nuns' songs** | **Therigatha** (Buddhist nuns) | — |
| **Saints' songs** | **Theragatha** (Buddhist monks) | — |

> 🎯 **Key Trap:** Therigatha = **Buddhist** text (nuns' songs in Khuddaka Nikaya); Nettipakarana = **Buddhist**; Avadanashataka = **Buddhist**
> ✅ Parishishtaparvan and Trishashtilakshana Mahapurana = **Jaina** texts

## 45. SECTS COMPARISON — BUDDHISM vs JAINISM

| Aspect | Buddhism | Jainism |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **Major split** | **Hinayana vs Mahayana** (formalised at 4th Council under Kanishka) | **Digambara vs Shvetambara** (3rd century BCE) |
| **Trigger for split** | Dispute over monastic practices (2nd Council); Mahayana innovations | Magadha famine — Bhadrabahu migrated South vs Sthalabahu stayed back |
| **Sub-sects** | Hinayana → Theravada, Sarvastivadin, Sautrantika, Sammitiya; Mahayana → Madhyamika, Yogachara; Vajrayana | Digambara → Bisapantha, Terapantha, Taranapantha, Gumanapantha, Totapantha; Shvetambara → Murtipujaka, Sthanakvasi, Terapanthi |
| **On idol worship** | Hinayana = NO idol worship; Mahayana = YES idol worship | Digambara Bisapantha/Terapantha = YES; Digambara Taranapantha = NO; Shvetambara Sthanakvasi/Terapanthi = NO |
| **On women** | Admitted with 8 restrictive rules | Digambara = women CANNOT attain moksha in this birth; Shvetambara = women CAN attain moksha |
| **Council presidents** | Mahakassapa (1st), Sabbakami (2nd), Moggaliputta Tissa (3rd), Vasumitra (4th) | Sthulabhadra (1st), Devardhigani (2nd) |
| **Royal patrons** | Ajatashatru (1st council), Ashoka (3rd), Kanishka (4th) | No major royal patrons for councils; Chandragupta Maurya (personal patron) |
| **Survival** | Declined and nearly disappeared from India | Survived and still strong in India |
