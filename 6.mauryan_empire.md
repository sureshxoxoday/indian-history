## 1. INTRODUCTION & SIGNIFICANCE

- Maurya period = **remarkable period in the early history of Indian subcontinent**
- Marks establishment of the **first sub-continental empire**
- Also marks development of **innovative and comparatively stable governance strategies**
- Extent: Ruled over the whole of the sub-continent **leaving Kerala, Tamil Nadu and some parts of north-eastern India**
- Period: **c. 324–187 BCE**
- Capital: **Pataliputra** (modern Patna)

> 💡 **Memory tip:** Mauryas = India's **first empire** + **first stable governance system**

## 2. SOURCES FOR THE MAURYAN PERIOD

### Primary Literary Sources (Most Important)
- **Kautilya's Arthashastra** — political treatise by Chandragupta's minister
- **Megasthenes' Indica** — account of Greek ambassador to Mauryan court
- **Junagarh Inscription of Rudradaman I** — attributes beginning of construction of Sudarshana lake to Chandragupta's reign
- **Edicts issued by Ashoka** — throw clearest light on history of this period

### Other Major Literary Sources

| Source | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Hemachandra's Parishishtaparvan** | Establishes Chandragupta's connections with Jainism |
| **Vishakhadatta's Mudrarakshasa** | 5th century CE (Gupta period); historical drama describing clever machinations of Chanakya against Chandragupta's enemies |
| **Dandin's Dashakumaracharita** | Literary source for Mauryan period |
| **Banabhatta's Kadambari** | Literary source |
| **Mahavamsa, Milindapanho, Mahabhashya** | Trinity of Buddhist texts giving account of Chandragupta's life |
| **Dipavamsa, Ashokavadana, Divyavadana** | Buddhist texts giving account of Ashoka |
| **Vamsatthapakasini** | 10th century commentary on legend of Chanakya and Chandragupta |

## 3. KAUTILYA'S ARTHASHASTRA

### Basic Facts
- Considered one of the **most sophisticated and detailed treatises** ever produced in India
- **Title meaning:** *Arthashastra* = Sanskrit word literally meaning **"the science of material wellbeing"**; also accepted as **"the science of statecraft"**
- According to Arthashastra: **artha** (material well-being) is superior to both *dharma* (spiritual well-being) and *kama* (sensual pleasure)
- Author: **Kautilya**, also known as **Chanakya** or **Vishnugupta** — Chandragupta Maurya's chief minister
- Compared to Italian Renaissance writer **Niccolo Machiavelli** (author of *The Prince*)
- **Lost for centuries** — rediscovered in India in **c. 1905 CE** by **R. Shamashastry** (written on palm leaves)
- Edition dated to approximately **c. 250 CE** — many centuries after time of Kautilya, but main ideas are generally accepted as Kautilya's

### Structure of Arthashastra
- Contains **15 books (Adhikaranas)**:
  - First **5 books** = deal with *tantra* (internal administration)
  - Next **8 books** = deal with *avapa* (interstate relations)
  - Final **2 books** = other miscellaneous topics

### Key Topics Covered
- Diplomacy and war tactics (two most detailed points)
- Law, prisons, taxation, irrigation, agriculture
- Mining, fortifications, coinage, manufacturing, trade, administration and **spies**

### Kautilya's Philosophy
- Ideas are **completely practical and unsentimental**
- Writes openly about: assassinations, killing family members, managing secret agents, violating treaties, spying on ministers
- Also writes about **moral duty of the king** and stresses on **paternal despotism**
- Famous quote: *"The happiness of the subjects is the happiness of the king; their welfare is his. His own pleasure is not his good but the pleasure of his subjects is his good"*

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (Practice Q):** Chanakya wrote Indica and Arthashastra — **FALSE** ❌ Chanakya wrote **Arthashastra**; **Indica** was written by **Megasthenes**

## 4. MEGASTHENES' INDICA

### Basic Facts
- **Indica** = account left by **Greek ambassador Megasthenes**, sent by **Seleucus Nikator** to the court of **Chandragupta Maurya**
- Megasthenes lived in the Mauryan capital **Pataliputra**
- The book has **NOT survived** — fragments preserved in later Greek and Latin works:
  - **Diodorus** (historian who describes Alexander's Indian campaign)
  - **Strabo** (geographer whose books deal with geography of India and Persia)
  - **Pliny** (Roman scholar who cites Megasthenes' observations)
  - **Arrian** (statesman and soldier who provides account of Alexander's campaign and Admiral Nearchus's voyage back to Babylon)

### What Indica Describes
- India is a **quadrilateral-shaped country** bound by ocean on southern and eastern side
- Sub-continent in terms of size and shape
- Soil, climate, rivers, plants, animals
- Contains lengthy descriptions of **elephants, horses and monkeys**
- Produce, administration, society, **legends and folklore**
- Greeks referred to Indians' worship of Lord Krishna as **Dionysus** and **Herakles/Hercules**
- Portrayed Indians as **honest** (since theft was rare) and having great character and moral values
- Megasthenes was **greatly impressed** by the contentment and prosperity of the Indian people

### Famous Quote from Indica:
> *"Famine has never visited India and that there has never been a general scarcity in the supply of nourishing food and even in times of war the Indians would never ravage the cultivated soil as was done by other nations but would fight their battles, away from agricultural fields allowing the tillers of the soil to carry on cultivation even when battles were raging."*

### Faults of Megasthenes' Work
- **Mistakes in details**
- **Uncritical acceptance of Indian folklore**
- **Tendency to idealise Indian culture** by standards of Greek philosophy
- Influenced by Herodotus' classification of Egyptian society into seven classes — mentioned **seven castes in India** (wrong — contrary to fourfold varna system)
  - However, correctly identified two important aspects: **endogamy** and **hereditary occupation**
- **Strabo also wrongly states** Indians were ignorant of writing and smelting, and never drank wine except at sacrifices

## 5. THE MAURYAN DYNASTY — RULERS

### Origins of the Maurya Family
- **Ambiguous origins** — varies text to text
- **Buddhist texts** (Digha Nikaya, Mahavamsa, Divyavadana): Mauryas belong to branch of Kshatriya **Moriya clan** associated with Shakyas — to give family higher status
- **Brahmanical sources**: Consider them Shudras and heretics — each king was strong patron of a heterodox sect
- **Vishnu Purana**: Chandragupta was eldest son of Maurya, son of Nanda king Sarvarthasiddhi, by Mura (daughter of a hunter — *vrishala*)
- Family also believed associated with the **Nandas**

---

### Chandragupta Maurya (324/321–297 BCE)

| Fact | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Founded** | Mauryan dynasty with help of mentor **Chanakya/Kautilya** |
| **Strategy** | First established in **Punjab**, then moved eastwards to gain control over Magadha region |
| **Strategy lesson** | Adopted strategy from a moral drawn from a woman scolding child for eating from centre of dish (bound to be hotter than sides) — start from periphery, not centre |
| **Greek name** | Called **Sandrocottus** by Greek scholars (identified by **William Jones**) |
| **Alexander connection** | Greek sources suggest a meeting between Chandragupta and Alexander (who invaded north-west India during 327–326 BCE) |
| **Seleucus Nikator** | Defeated Seleucus around **301 BCE**; Seleucus compelled to yield **Arachosia** (Qandahar area, south-east Afghanistan), **Gedrosia** (south Baluchistan), **Parapomisadai** (area between Afghanistan and Indian sub-continent — Kabul, Herat, Gandhara) in return for **500 elephants** |
| **Ambassador** | Seleucus sent **Megasthenes** (who wrote the Indica) as ambassador to Mauryan court |
| **Empire** | Chief architect of Mauryan empire; built vast empire including Bihar, Nepal, western and north-western India and the Deccan |
| **Religion** | According to Jain texts, adopted **Jainism**; went to hills of **Shravana Belagola** (near Mysore) with **Bhadrabahu**; committed **Sallekhana** (death by slow starvation) |

| Fact | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Greek name** | **Amitrochates** (destroyer of foes) — Greek writers; **Amitraghat** (killer of enemies) — Mahabhasya |

### Ashoka (268–232 BCE)

#### Succession & Background
- After Bindusara's death in **273 BCE**, there was a **four-year succession conflict**
- According to Divyavadana: Bindusara wanted his son **Susima** to succeed him; Ashoka was choice of Bindusara's ministers
- With help of minister named **Radhagupta**, after killing **99 brothers**, Ashoka finally acquired Magadha throne
- Four years later came his **formal consecration** (hence 273 BCE death, 268 BCE consecration)
- Appointed **Viceroy of Taxila and Ujjain** (both commercial cities) during Bindusara's reign

#### Names/Titles of Ashoka

| Name | Source/Meaning |
|------|---------------|
| **Buddhashakya** | In the Maski edict |
| **Ashoka** | In the Maski edict |
| **Dharmasoka** | Sarnath inscription |
| **Devanampiya** | Meaning "beloved of the gods" |
| **Piyadassi** | Meaning "pleasing appearance" — given in Sri Lankan Buddhist chronicles Dipavamsa and Mahavamsa |

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (UPSC 1995):** The name by which Ashoka is generally referred to in his inscriptions — **Answer: Piyadassi** ✅ (NOT Chakravarti, NOT Dharmadeva, NOT Dharmakirti)

#### Ashoka's Family
- **Mother**: According to Ashokavadana, daughter of a Brahmana of Champa named **Subhadrangi**
- **Name origin**: Literally meaning "without sorrow" — from his mother's exclamation "I am now without sorrow" at his birth, which reunited his parents separated by palace intrigue
- **Wife Mahadevi**: Love marriage with daughter of a merchant of **Vidisha** → mother of **Mahendra/Mahinda** and **Sanghamitra/Sanghamitta** (celebrated children who helped propagate Buddhism)
- **Queens mentioned**: Asandhimitta, Padmavati, **Tissarakhita** (tried to get Bodhi tree cut — only queen mentioned in Queen's Edict/Allahabad-Kosam pillar edict), **Karuvaki** (described as mother of prince **Tivara** — only son of Ashoka mentioned by name in inscriptions)

#### Ashoka's Key Achievements
- Regarded as one of the **greatest kings of all times**
- Probably the **first ruler to maintain direct contact with his people** through inscriptions
- **Hellenistic contemporaries**: Antiochius II of Syria, Ptolemy II of Egypt, Antigonus of Macedonia, Magas of Cyrenaica (Libya), Alexander of Epirus — all mentioned in edicts of Ashoka
- **Converted to Buddhism**: According to tradition, converted by his nephew **Nigrodha** (who became monk at age of 7); according to Divyavadana, by **Samudra** (merchant-turned-monk)
- Buddhism went **outside India for the first time** during his reign
- Sent son **Mahendra** and daughter **Sanghamitra** to **Ceylon (Sri Lanka)**
- Appointed **Dharma Mahamattas** to propagate dharma among various social groups including women (in **14th year** of his reign)
- Organised **third Buddhist council at Pataliputra** (in **18th year** of his reign); after which sent Buddhist missionaries to Ceylon and Suvarnabhumi
- In **21st year** of his reign during second Dharmayatra tour, visited **Lumbini** (birthplace of Buddha)
- **Banned animal sacrifice**, regulated slaughter of animals for food
- Established **dharmashalas, hospitals and sarais** throughout his kingdom

#### Brihadratha — Last Maurya King
- Maurya empire declined rapidly after Ashoka; later kings had very short reigns
- Empire became weak and fragmented
- Last king **Brihadratha** was killed by his military commander **Pushyamitra Sunga** in **187 BCE**
- → **Sunga Dynasty** (187 BCE)

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (Practice Q — Image 12):** Dashratha was the last Maurya king who was killed by Pushyamitra Sunga — **FALSE** ❌ It was **Brihadratha**, NOT Dasharatha ✅

## 6. ASHOKAN INSCRIPTIONS AND ASHOKA'S DHAMMA

### Overview of Inscriptions
- History of Ashoka reconstructed primarily on basis of his **inscriptions**
- These inscriptions throw light on: his reign, external and domestic policies, views about dhamma, extent of his empire
- **Total 33 inscriptions** — remarkable documents vividly describing ambience of his time
- **Five types** of inscriptions:
  1. Major Rock Edicts
  2. Minor Rock Edicts
  3. Separate Rock Edicts
  4. Major Pillar Edicts
  5. Minor Pillar Edicts

### Languages and Scripts Used
- **Brahmi** — used in most inscriptions (deciphered by **James Prinsep** in **c. 1837 CE**)
- **Kharosthi** — used in north-west border areas (written **right to left**)
- **Prakrit** — widely used
- **Aramaic** — used in north-west border (Shar-i-Kuna Greek-Aramaic inscription); interestingly some concepts drawn from **Zoroastrian philosophical discourses**
- **Greek** — used in north-west border

> 📌 Interestingly, **no reference to teachings of Buddha** in Greek and Aramaic versions
> 📌 Ashoka's inscriptions do **NOT contain** certain key Buddhist ideas: Eight-Fold Path, Four Noble Truths, goal of Nibbana

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (UPSC 1997):** Which ancient India script was written from right to left? — **Answer: Kharosthi** ✅

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (UPSC 2016):** Who among the following first deciphered the edicts of Emperor Ashoka? — **Answer: James Prinsep** ✅ (NOT Georg Buhler, NOT Max Muller, NOT William Jones — William Jones identified Sandrocottus as Chandragupta)

| Rock Edict | Corresponding Message |
|------------|----------------------|
| **First** | Declared prohibition of animal sacrifice |
| **Second** | Medical treatment of humans and animals; planting beneficial medicinal herbs and roots, fruits; digging of wells |

| **Fourth** | *Bheri Ghosha* (war drum) replaced by *Dhamma Ghosha*; King Ashoka attached greatest value to this duty |
| **Fifth** | Appointment of **Dhamma Mahamattas** — special cadre of officials entrusted to spread dhamma within the kingdom |
| **Sixth** | *Mantri Parishad* and officers like *Pulisani* and *Pativedakas* (reporters) |
| **Seventh** | Religious tolerance amongst all sects and welfare measures for public — not only in own kingdom but also in neighbouring kingdoms of Cholas, Pandyas and that of Antiochus in north-west |
| **Eighth** | Royal pleasure tours (*Vihara-yatras*) replaced by *dhamma* tours (*dhamma-yatras*). Ashoka himself went to **Sambodhi at Bodhgaya** |
| **Ninth** | Criticism of uselessness of various ceremonies; stress on dhamma and moral conduct |
| **Tenth** | King desires no more fame or glory |

| **Fourteenth** | Purpose of rock edicts |

### Pillar Edicts and Their Corresponding Messages

**Features of Ashokan Pillars:**
- Signify the **axis of the world** (*axis mundi*) that separated heaven and earth
- Found at: **Lauriya-Araraj, Lauriya Nandangarh, Rampurva, Nigali Sagar, Sarnath, Topra and Meerut**
- Two pillars shifted to **Delhi** by **Feroz Shah Tughlaq** (one from Topra, one from Meerut)
- Pillars **without inscriptions**: bull capital at Rampurva, lion capital at Vaishali, Kolam pillar

**Physical Features of Pillars:**
- Made of **sandstone quarried from Chunar** (near Mirzapur in UP)
- Mostly **monolithic** (carved out of single piece of stone)
- Have **lustrous, polished surface** (as shining as Northern Black Polished Ware — NBPW)
- Do **NOT have a base** — plain smooth cylindrical shaft tapers slightly upward to height of **12–14 m**
- Topped by **bell capital** (stone carved in shape of inverted lotus)
- On top of bell capital is **platform (abacus)** — intended to support crowned animal
- **Abacus**: square and plain in earlier pillars; circular and curved in later ones

**Animal Capital Symbolism:**

| Animal/Symbol | Symbolism |
|--------------|-----------|
| **Lotus** | Purity and fecundity in Indian tradition |
| **Lion** | Solar symbol; represents the Buddha (*Sakya-simha* = lion among the Sakyas) |
| **Bull** | Symbol of fertility |
| **Elephant** | Relevant in Buddhism — Buddha entered his mother's womb in form of white elephant |
| **Spoked wheel** | Represents *dharmachakra* — wheel of dharma; also associated with sovereignty |
| **Horse** | Symbolises departure of Siddhartha from his home |

| Pillar Edict | Corresponding Message |
|-------------|----------------------|
| **First** | Social code |
| **Second** | Describes dhamma as consisting of least amount of sin, performing virtuous deeds, having compassion, purity and truthfulness |
| **Third** | Soul and sin |
| **Fourth** | Rajukas (also introduces *samata* — fairness — in judicial procedures) |
| **Fifth** | **Delhi-Topra pillar edict** — prohibitions regarding animal killing |
| **Sixth** | Welfare of people |
| **Seventh** | Dhamma Mahamattas |

### Ashoka's Dhamma

- Most of Ashoka's inscriptions are about **dhamma** (Prakrit form of *dharma*)
- Literally meaning: **universal law or righteousness or social and religious order**
- Clearly inspired by Buddhist *upasaka dhamma* (Buddha's teaching for laity) but was **NOT a mirror image** of it
- Ashoka's dhamma was a set of teachings that could **NOT be identified with just narrow sectarian belief**
- Rather it encompassed a **social and intellectual code of ethics** influencing many aspects of social life
- Emphasised on: **non-violence, mutual respect and understanding** between people of different sects and beliefs
- Ashoka did NOT see dhamma as **piety** resulting from good deeds inspired by formal religious beliefs, but as **conformity to a social ethic**
- His dhamma was aimed at creating attitude of mind having **primacy of ethical behaviour** and recognition of **dignity of human life**
- He repeatedly asked people to extend: **respect, consideration, compassion and tolerance towards slaves and servants; respect for teachers; obedience to parents; generosity towards friends and relatives; regard and donations to Brahmanas and Shramanas; concern for all living beings and abstention from destroying life**
- Basic attributes of dhamma: **compassion (daya), charity (dana), truthfulness, purity and gentleness**

> 📌 **Important**: Ashoka's inscriptions do NOT contain Eight-Fold Path, Four Noble Truths, or goal of Nibbana — showing dhamma was NOT simply Buddhism

## 7. ASHOKA'S LEGACY

### Path-breaking Achievements

**(a) Political unification of the country:**
- Not only added Kalinga to Mauryan dynasty
- Also bound whole country together by **one dhamma, one language** (Prakrit) and practically **one script** (Brahmi — used in most inscriptions)

**(b) Spread of tolerance and respect:**
- Adopted and preached tolerance in religious sphere
- Did NOT try to force his Buddhist faith on subjects
- Made gifts to **non-Buddhist sects** as well (donated **Barabara caves to Ajivika ascetics**)
- Respected other scripts: Kharosthi, Aramaic, Greek
- Respected other languages: Greek, Prakrit, Sanskrit

**(c) Promotion of cultural contacts:**
- Brought innovative administrative changes
- Promoted cultural contacts between Indian states and between India and outer world
- It would NOT be exaggeration to call Ashoka **"the first global cultural ambassador of India"**

**(d) Policy of peace and non-aggression:**
- Famous for his policy of **peace, non-conquest and non-aggression**
- Much ahead of his time and generation

## 8. MAURYAN ADMINISTRATION — SAPTANGA THEORY

### Overview
- Maurya period marked by **innovative administrative changes** and an **elaborate, highly centralised bureaucratic rule**
- **King as fountainhead of all powers** — claimed **paternal despotism** rather than divine rule
- Ashoka's empire divided into **provinces** with a **viceroy** in each province
- Kautilya's concept of **Saptanga Rajya** — a state comprises **seven inter-related and interlocked angas or prakritis (elements)**:

| # | Element | Sanskrit | Description |
|---|---------|----------|-------------|
| 1 | **Svamin** | King | Soul of all seven elements |
| 2 | **Amatya** | Ministers | All high officials, counsellors, executive heads of departments |
| 3 | **Janapada** | Territory and population | The land and its people |
| 4 | **Durga** | Fortified capital | The military and defence |
| 5 | **Kosha** | Treasury | Revenue and economic resources |
| 6 | **Danda/Bala** | Justice or force | Judicial and punitive power |
| 7 | **Mitra** | Ally | Foreign policy and diplomatic relations |

> 🎯 **PYQ Alert (Practice Q):** Kautilya laid down a Saptanga theory of administration — **TRUE** ✅

---

### 1. Svamin (King)

- **Dharmapravakta** = promulgator of social orders (issued *Rajasasana* = royal rescripts; maintained *porana pakiti* = ancient rules and customs)
- Assisted by council of ministers in daily affairs; yet took **all final decisions** regarding revenue, law and order, war, or any other administrative matter
- Expected to be **agile and accessible** to officials at all times
- Ashoka declared: **"all his subjects were like his children"** and desired *"their happiness in this and the other world"*
- Arthashastra asserts king should maintain helpless children, old people, childless women and other destitute and vulnerable people
- King also ensured people's welfare by **planting trees along roads, digging wells, and providing medical care** for both men and animals
- Female personal bodyguards of the king — **skilled in archery**
- **Spy system**: *Sanstha* (stationary spies posted permanently at particular region) and *Sanchara* (wandering spies who wandered from place to place)
- Chandragupta maintained body of spies called **Overseers** (*Episcopoi* of Diodorus or *Ephori* of Strabo and Arrian)
- Also mention of **Pativedakas** and **Pulisani** — special reporters of the king

> 💡 Famous Megasthenes quote about Chandragupta: *"Chandragupta was a very hard-working official. He remained in court whole day, did not sleep at day time and even when he would have his body massaged or his hair combed and dressed, he would attend to public business and gave audience to his ambassadors."*

### 2. Amatya (Ministers)

**Council of Ministers:**
- King appointed council of ministers to assist him in day-to-day administration called **Mantriparishad**
- Mantriparishad was a larger body compared to the **Mantra-parishad** (small consultative body of mantris)
- According to Megasthenes, Diodorus, Starbo, Arrian — Mantriparishad was **very influential**
- Besides advising king in administration, it exercised great influence in appointment of governors, viceroys, deputy governors, treasurers, generals, admirals, judges, chief magistrates and other high officials
- Meeting attended by **Maha-Mantrins** (high ministers) — handsomely paid
- *Mantrins* received **48,000 panas** salary per annum
- *Mantriparishad* members received only **12,000 panas** per annum

**Bodies of Nikayas (trained officials):**
- Looked after ordinary affairs of the realm

**Eighteen Tirthas** (highest functionaries at centre):
- **Mantrin** (chief minister)
- **Purohita** (high priest)
- **Yuvaraja** (heir apparent)
- **Senapati** (commander-in-chief)

**Other Officials:**
- **Amatyas** = filled highest administrative and judicial appointments
- **Mahamattas** = and Adhyakshas (superintendents — called *Astynomoi*, i.e. magistrates of Strabo)
- **27 Adhyakshas** responsible for running various economic departments: agriculture, mining, weaving, trade, etc.
- **Nagaradhyaksha** = magistrates who looked after towns and cities
- **Baladhyakshas** = those looking after military

**Important Salary Ratio:**
- Clerk's/soldier's salary to chief minister/commander of army = **1:96** — enormous gap

**Key Executive Officials:**
- **Samahartri** = chief collector of revenue (most important executive official; maintained accounts and collected taxes from all types of sources)
- **Samnidhatri** = treasurer; also in charge of royal stores, *akshapatala* (records-cum-audit office) and *dauvarika* (chief of palace attendants)

**Tests for Amatya Appointment (Kautilya):**
- Only those whose character tested under all kinds of allurements were promoted to higher positions of advisers
- Amatyas in charge of civil and criminal justice: purified by **religious tests** (*Dharmopadhasuddha*)
- Appointed as chancellor of exchequer (Samaharta): purified by **money test** (*Arthopadhasuddha*)
- Those appointed to pleasure gardens: purified by **love test** (*Kamopadhasuddha*)
- Those employed in work needing bold and immediate steps: purified by **fear test** (*Bhayopadhasuddha*)

## 9. PROVINCIAL AND LOCAL ADMINISTRATION

### 3. Janapada (Territory and Population)

**Provinces:**
- Apart from Magadha (capital Pataliputra), Mauryan Empire divided into **four other provinces** (five total under Ashoka):

| Province | Capital |
|----------|---------|
| **Uttarapatha** | Taxila (north-western India) |
| **Dakshinapatha** | Suvarnagiri (southern India) |
| **Eastern India** | Tosali |
| **Avantiratha** | Ujjain (western India) |
| **Kalinga** | Tasali/Dhauli (added during Ashoka's reign) |

- **Viceroy** = head of provincial administration; in charge of law and order and collection of taxes for centre
- Generally a prince from royal family (*kumara* or *aryaputra*)
- Assisted by **Mahamattas** and a council of ministers

**Hierarchy of Administrative Units:**

| Unit | Size | Official |
|------|------|---------|
| *Sthaniya* | 800 villages | — |
| *Drona* | 400 villages | — |
| *Mukha* | 200 villages | — |
| *Kharvatika* | 10 villages | — |
| *Samgrahana* | — | — |

**Divisional level:**
- Provinces divided into **divisions** headed by **Pradeshikas** (no advisory council)
- Divisions divided into **districts** under **Rajukas** (*derived from rajju* meaning rope — referring to land measurement using ropes)
- Rajukas assisted by **Yuktas** (clerks) in accounting, secretarial and miscellaneous works

**District level:**
- Districts divided into groups of **5 or 10 villages** headed by **Sthanikas** (collected taxes)
- Assisted by **Gopas** (maintained proper records and accounts)

**City level:**
- **Nagrika** = city administrative chief
- **Pradeshtha** = city judge
- **Rakshin** = police chief in the city

**Village level (lowest unit):**
- Village headed by **Gramini/Gramika** on consultation of *Grama-vriddhas* (village elders)

> 📌 Maurya administration was structurally like a **pyramid** — Gramini at bottom, king at top
> 📌 Central province of Magadha = under strict supervision of king; far-flung areas may have had varied administrative control

## 10. MUNICIPAL ADMINISTRATION OF PATALIPUTRA

- Municipal administration of Pataliputra was of **unique character**
- Conducted by **six committees of five members each** (30 members total, according to both Megasthenes and Arthashastra)

| Committee | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| **1. Industry** | Look after production of commodities; keep watch on quality of raw materials; decide fair value; stamp finished articles as evidence of suitability for market |
| **2. Foreigners** | Strabo and Diodorus state Mauryan Government took special care of foreigners; duty = ensure no foreigner was wronged; send physicians to attend any sick foreigner; if foreigner died, bury him and deliver property to family |
| **3. Vital Statistics (birth and death registration)** | Enquire when and how deaths occurred; register every birth and death; not only levied a tax but also ensured birth and death among both high and low may not escape cognizance of government |
| **4. Trade, commerce and market regulations** | Kept watch over weights and measures; ensured commodities sold before quality deteriorated; seasonal products sold by public notice; no one allowed to deal in more than one commodity (but could by paying double/triple tax); dues in grain kept as buffer stock in food shortage |
| **5. Manufactured articles** | Supervised public notice given for sale of manufactured articles; strict watch that newly manufactured articles not mixed or piled with old stock |
| **6. Tax collection** | In charge of collecting **one-tenth of prices** of articles or produce sold as tax; fraud in payment punishable with **death penalty** |

## 11. MILITARY — DURGA (FORTIFIED CAPITAL)

### Army Size
- According to Greek writer **Justin**: Chandragupta had **6,00,000 infantry, 30,000 cavalry, 9,000 elephants and 8,000 chariots** (exaggerated but large army cannot be doubted)

### Army Officials
- **Anta-Mahamattas** = responsible for security of frontier forts
- **Senapati** and **Nayaks** = important military officers
- Under them: several **Adhyakshas/superintendents** of different wings and units

### War Office
- Administration of different branches of army carried through a **War Office comprising 30 members** (obvious experts in different branches of military art and science)
- Divided into **six boards of five members each:**

| Board | Head |
|-------|------|
| **(i) Board of Admiralty** | In charge of **navy** (interestingly Kautilya does NOT mention navy) |
| **(ii) Board of Infantry** | Headed by **Padadhyaksha** |
| **(iii) Board of Cavalry** | Headed by **Asvadhyaksha** |
| **(iv) Board of War Chariots** | Headed by **Rathadhyaksha** |
| **(v) Board of War Elephants** | Headed by **Hastyadhyaksha** |
| **(vi) Board of Transport and Supervision of Equipment** | — |

### Types of Troops
- **Maula** = hereditary warriors
- **Bhartiakas** = mercenaries
- **Forest tribe soldiers and allies** (furnished by friends)

### Kautilya's Fort Recommendations
- Troops should be stationed along approaches to fort
- Fort walls surrounded by **not one but three moats** filled with lotuses and crocodiles
- Fort should have plenty of supplies and **numerous secret escape routes**
- Strongly approves of **standing army** — recruited from all four varnas; adequately trained and maintained by state

## 12. ECONOMY AND TREASURY (KOSHA)

### Agriculture (Main Source of Revenue)
- **Mainstay of economy** in this period was agriculture (animal husbandry and trade were other main occupations)
- **Land revenue** = main source of income
- Principal crops: rice, barley, millet, wheat, sugarcane, pulses, peas, oilseeds

### Land Revenue System
- Notion of **private property of Kshetra** (land which could be sold under certain regulations) emerged and got established during this time
- **Kshetrika** (land-owner) distinguished from **Upasava** (tenant)
- In land disputes where neither side could prove claim: property went to **king**
- Peasants paid **one-sixth of produce** as **bhaga** and extra tax **bali** as tribute
- **Bali** = land tax/tax on area of land = levied at rate of **one-sixth of produce** = main item of revenue
- Other taxes:
  - **Pindakara** (assessed on groups of villages)
  - **Hiranya** (paid only in cash)
  - **Kara** (levied on fruits and flower gardens)
  - **Udaka-bhaga** (water rate — for irrigated land; levied at **one-fifth to one-third** of produce)
  - **Pranaya** = emergency taxes or additional levies state could impose if treasury got depleted

### State Agricultural Settlements (Shunyanivesha)
- Mauryan state founded new agricultural settlements to bring virgin land under cultivation
- People from overpopulated areas, slaves and prisoners of war were brought to work on fields
- These villages belonged to king and were looked after by government officials called **Sitadhyaksha** (Superintendent of agriculture)

### Share-cropping
- Existed on both state-owned and privately-owned land
- **Ardhasitikas** = kept half of produce (state-owned land)
- **Svaviryopajivins** = kept one-fourth or one-fifth of produce

### Trade and Industry
- Trade and urban economy received great impetus under Mauryas
- Main centres of **textile manufacturing**: Varanasi, Mathura, Bengal, Gandhara and Ujjain
- State functioned as **entrepreneur** with state-run textile workshops under **Sutradhyaksha**
- Chariot workshops under **Rathadhyaksha**
- **Mining and metallurgy** important; mine officer called **Akaradhyaksha**
- Kautilya describes forests, pastures and mines as **state property**
- Trade conducted through **land and river routes**; Pataliputra connected through various trade routes
- Main centre of trade in north-west: **Taxila** (connected with central Asian markets)
- Important seaports: **Tamralipti** (Tamluk in West Bengal) in east; **Broach** in west
- Guilds of traders and artisans called **Shrenis** (or guilds or *puga*) — headed by **Jesthaka**
- Mauryas introduced **iron on large scale** in different parts; maintained **monopoly over production of iron** — done through official called **Loha-adyaksha**

### Important Trade Officials
| Official | Role |
|---------|------|
| **Panyadhyaksha** | Officer in charge of trade, price fixation and sale of goods produced by state-run manufacturing units |
| **Sansthadhyaksha** | Superintendent of markets |
| **Rupadarshaka** | Inspector of coins |
| **Pautavadhyaksha** | In charge of standardised weights and measures |
| **Sutradhyaksha** | In charge of state-run textile workshops |
| **Akaradhyaksha** | Mine officer |
| **Loha-adyaksha** | In charge of iron production (monopoly) |
| **Sitadhyaksha** | Superintendent of agriculture |

### Currency
- **Punch-marked silver coins** carrying symbol of **peacock, hill and regent** = imperial currency of Mauryans
- Urban taxes included **Shulka** (duties on imported and exported goods) and excise duties on local manufactures

### Expenditure
- Considerable part of revenue spent on: **maintenance of army, war horses, war elephants and war chariots**
- Artisans also received maintenance from royal exchequer
- Herdsmen paid grains from royal granary for clearing land of wild beasts and fowls
- Brahmanas and Shramanas received unused land as royal gifts (but such land could **not be sold or mortgaged** by beneficiaries)

## 13. JUSTICE — DANDA/BALA

### Judicial System
- **King** = supreme authority; personally adjudicated cases before him
- He would **never keep his petitioners waiting**
- Kautilya quote: *"when in the court, he (the king) shall never cause his petitioners to wait at the door, for when a king makes himself inaccessible to the people and entrusts his work to his immediate officers, he may be sure to engender confusion in business, and to cause thereby public disaffection, and himself a prey to his enemies"*

### Courts and Officials
- **Dharmasthas** = judges (also city Mahamattas given judicial functions by Ashokan inscriptions)
- **Pradeshtris** = officers responsible for suppression of criminals
- Various civil and criminal courts at local level from **village to province**
- Most cases disposed off at **village level by village elders**
- **Police headquarters** in all principal centres
- **Bandhanagara** = proper jail
- **Charka** = police lock-up

### Punishment System
- Punishments very **severe**: decapitation, amputation of limbs, fines and forfeitures
- Nature of punishment depended on: **nature, gravity, circumstances** and also on **varna of offender and plaintiff**
- **Dvija/higher varnas** = given lighter punishment than lower ones
- **Pillar Edict 4**: Ashoka claims he had introduced **samata** (fairness) in judicial procedures
- Same edict refers to a **three-day respite** for those condemned to death to appeal the decision
- This indicates **death penalty was present** even during Ashoka's time

### Four Sources of Law (Kautilya)
1. **Dharma** (sacred law)
2. **Vyavahara** (usage)
3. **Charitam** (custom and precedents)
4. **Rajasasana** (royal edicts or orders)

## 14. FOREIGN POLICY — MITRA (ALLY)

### Shad-Gunya (Six Policies)
From the point of **Vijigishu** (the would-be-conqueror), Kautilya lists six policies a king should follow:

| Policy | When to Use |
|--------|-------------|
| **Sandhi** (peace treaty) | If one is **weaker** than the enemy |
| **Vigraha** (hostility) | If one is **stronger** than the enemy |
| **Asana** (keeping quiet) | If one's power is **equal** to that of enemy |
| **Yana** (marching on military expedition) | If one is **much stronger** than enemy |
| **Samshraya** (seeking shelter with another king or in a fort) | If one is **very weak** |
| **Dvaidhibhava** (double policy — sandhi with one, vigraha with another) | If one can fight enemy **with help of an ally** |

### Diplomatic Posts (Arthashastra)
- **Nisriharthaduta** = Full-fledged ambassador
- **Parimitarthaduta** = Could NOT go beyond his instruction in negotiation
- **Sasanharaduta** = Special messenger

> 📌 Mauryas probably had a **full-fledged department of foreign affairs**

## 15. MAURYAN SOCIETY

- Despite the challenge posed by Buddhism and Jainism, **Brahmanas and Kshatriyas dominated** the social hierarchy
- **Varna system continued to exist**
- As a result of greater trade and commerce, improvement in social status of **Vaishyas or trading communities** and **Shudras**
- Now Shudras could be involved in **agricultural and artisanal activities**
- This period also saw **increase in number of untouchables** — *Chandalas* and *Shvapakas* (dog breeders), also called *Antavasayin* (literally meaning "living at the end")
- Surprisingly, **no mention of either Varna or Sati** in Ashokan edicts

### Slavery in Mauryan India
- Megasthenes lauded Indian society for NOT having slaves — yet **ironically, slavery DID exist**
- **Arthashastra** says: "no Arya or freeman could be reduced to slavery"
- References to **dasas** (slaves) in Ashokan inscriptions testify to existence of slavery
- Ashokan edicts, while discussing dhamma, demands **courteous behaviour** towards dasas and **bhatakas/bhritakas** (servants)
- Kautilya lists various rules and penalties for treatment of male and female slaves:
  - A man could NOT be slave by birth, by voluntarily selling himself, by being captured in war, or as result of judicial punishment
  - Certain punishments prescribed for selling or mortgaging a **pregnant woman slave** without arranging for her maternity
  - Reference of **manumission** (freeing slaves) on payment of money
  - If a dasi bore her master a son → she was freed AND child considered **father's legitimate child**

## 16. MAURYAN ART AND ARCHITECTURE

### Overview
- Mauryan power visible in **monumental stone sculptures and structures**
- Important beginnings in **rock-cut** and **stupa** architecture
- Stone culture dramatically emerged as **principal medium of Indian arts**
- Mauryan art strongly linked with **political and religious ideology** of that time
- Especially Ashoka patronised: stone sculptures, ring stones, disc stones, terracotta figurines and stupa architecture

### Stupas
- Ashoka played major role in popularising the **stupa cult**
- Not only redistributed Buddha's relics to every important town, but also ordered construction of stupas over them
- Estimated that Ashoka got built approximately **84,000 stupas**

### Ashokan Pillars — Greatest Innovation
- Ashoka's greatest innovation = **substitution of stone for wood and brick**
- Pillars at **Rampurva, Lauriya Nandangarh and Sarnath** = excellent examples of stone sculptures
- All pillars are **circular and monolithic**
- Made of **sandstone found at Chunar**, near Mirzapur in UP

### Rock-Cut Architecture
- The Maurya period saw beginning of **rock-cut architecture**
- **Lomasha Risi cave** in the **Barabar hills near Gaya** = magnificent rock-cut architecture
- **Majestic Dhauli caves** (near Bhubaneswar, Odisha) — contains rock sculpture of front part of an elephant
- **Famous Vajrasana** (throne of meditation) at Mahabodhi temple at Bodh Gaya probably belonged to this period

### Important Sculptures
- **Didarganj Yakshini** = most famous polished stone sculpture of a female; found at Didarganj (Patna area); *Yakshinis* are generally regarded as fertility deities and female counterpart of Yakshas (deities connected with water, trees, forest, wilderness and fertility)
- **Torso of a nude male figure** found at Lohanipur in Patna = another important polished Chunar sandstone sculpture
- **Stone portrait of Ashoka** found at **Kanaganahalli** (near Sannati, Karnataka) = also spectacular

### National Emblem
- Our **national emblem** adopted from the **four-lion capital** of Ashokan pillar at **Sarnath** near Benaras

### Universities and Learning Centres
- **Taxila, Ujjain and Varanasi** = famous centres of learning
- **Bhadrabahu's Kalpasutra** = attributed to the Mauryan period

### Wooden Architecture
- **Lomasha Risi and Sudama caves of Gaya** = great examples of wooden architectural heritage
- Megasthenes described grandeur of Mauryan palace at Pataliputra (wooden structure)
- Some remains including the **80-pillared hall** found at **Kumrahar** near Patna

### Material Culture
- Maurya period witnessed rapid development of **material culture in the Gangetic plains**
- New material culture based on intensive use of **iron and iron implements** (socketed axes, sickles, ploughshare, spoked wheel)
- **NBPW** (Northern Black Polished Ware) pottery artifacts
- Introduction of **burnt bricks** and timber in construction
- **Ring wells** (supplied water for domestic use; also served as soak pits)
- Existence of inscriptions, occasional NBPW potsherds and punch-marked coins in Bangladesh (Bogra district), Odisha (Sisupalgarh), Andhra (Amravati) and Karnataka

### Achaemenid/Persian Influence
- Mauryan art also believed to have **Achaemenid/Persian influence**
- Pillar inscriptions of Ashoka are very similar in form and style to **Persian king Darius' inscriptions**
- Words *lipi* and *dipi* occur in inscriptions of both Ashoka and Darius
- Both inscriptions begin in **third person** then move to first person

**Key Differences — Mauryan vs Persian Pillars:**

| Feature | Mauryan Pillars | Persian Pillars |
|---------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Base** | Absent — shaft rises directly from ground | Stand on bases (bell-shaped or plain rectangular/circular block) |
| **Lotus position** | Inverted lotus appears at **top of shaft** | Bell-shaped base at bottom |
| **Surface** | **Smooth** | **Fluted** |
| **Lotus bulge** | Typical bulge present | Bulge absent |
| **Abacus** | Present with independent animals carved in round | Absent in Persian context |

## 17. DECLINE OF THE MAURYAS

- Mauryan empire began to disintegrate soon after death of Ashoka around **232 BCE**
- Only one later Maurya, king **Dasaratha**, is known to have issued inscriptions
- Causes of decline:

### (a) Brahmanical Reaction
- Anti-sacrifice attitude of Buddhism and deriding of superfluous rituals by Ashoka brought **loss of income and livelihood** to Brahmanas (who lived on varied gifts made to them)
- Appointment of Dhamma Mahamattas would have come in **conflict with Brahmanas' prestige** as custodians of social morality and order
- Brahmanas made hostile — wanted ruler who would uphold their existing interests and privileges
- Interesting: new kingdoms arising on ruins of Maurya empire were ruled by Brahmanas — **Shungas and Kanvas** in Central India and **Satavahanas** in the Deccan
- Ashoka's **pacifist policy** also resulted in **lack of preparedness of the army** and may have been factor responsible for success of Greek invasion
- **Harprasad Shastri** corroborates this view — holds Ashoka's pro-Buddhist and pacifist policy responsible for annoying Brahmanas and resulting in fall of Mauryan empire

### (b) Oppressive Rule in Provinces
- Instances of complaints by citizens of certain provinces regarding misrule by **corrupt officials (dushtamatyas)**
- Kalinga edicts also point to Ashoka being very concerned about **oppression in provinces** — advises Mahamattas not to torture townsmen without due cause
- Disturbing fact: last Maurya king Brihadratha was **assassinated by his commander of army during an inspection of the troops** — not much hue and cry on murder → later Mauryas were **not very popular with the public**

### (c) Financial Crisis
- Enormous expenditure on maintaining large army and bureaucracy must have created **financial crisis**
- Cost of establishing settlements on newly cleared lands also strained treasury (people settling there initially exempted from tax)
- **D.D. Koshambi** supports this view — feels heavy economic pressure due to vast army and bureaucracy may have resulted in crisis

### (d) Spread of Material Culture
- Once knowledge of iron tools and weapons spread to peripheral areas, **Magadha lost its special advantage**
- New kingdoms founded on basis of material culture acquired from Magadha: **Shungas and Kanvas** (Central India), **Chetis** (Kalinga), **Satavahanas** (Deccan)

### (e) Neglect of North-West Frontier
- Neglect of safeguarding passage on north-west frontier cost Mauryans dearly
- 3rd century BCE: Central Asian nomadic tribes like **Scythians** were in state of flux
- Chinese ruler **Shih Huang Ti** (247–210 BCE) constructed **Great Wall of China** around **220 BCE** to protect against Scythian attack
- Such measures NOT taken by Ashoka on north-western frontier
- To escape Scythians, Parthians, Shakas and **Greeks were forced to move towards India**
- Greeks were **first to invade India in 206 BCE** and set up kingdom in north Afghanistan called **Bactria**
